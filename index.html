Critical Values

Flip card for interpretation guide

ABG Interpretation Steps

  1. Look at pH: Acidosis (<7.35) or Alkalosis (>7.45)
  2. Check PaCO₂: Respiratory component
    • High PaCO₂ (>45) = Respiratory acidosis
    • Low PaCO₂ (<35) = Respiratory alkalosis
  3. Check HCO₃⁻: Metabolic component
    • High HCO₃⁻ (>26) = Metabolic alkalosis
    • Low HCO₃⁻ (<22) = Metabolic acidosis
  4. Assess compensation:
    • Uncompensated: Abnormal pH
    • Partially compensated: pH abnormal but moving toward normal
    • Fully compensated: pH normal with abnormal PaCO₂ and HCO₃⁻
  5. Check oxygenation: PaO₂ and SaO₂

Tip: Use the tic-tac-toe method for quick assessment

  • pH <7.35 with PaCO₂ >45 = Respiratory acidosis
  • pH <7.35 with HCO₃⁻ <22 = Metabolic acidosis
  • pH >7.45 with PaCO₂ <35 = Respiratory alkalosis
  • pH >7.45 with HCO₃⁻ >26 = Metabolic alkalosis

SpO₂ for COPD Patients

For COPD patients, acceptable SpO₂ is typically 88-92%

When to Report Vital Signs

Vital Sign Report if:
Temperature < 36.0°C or > 38.5°C
Heart Rate < 50 or > 120 beats/min
Respiratory Rate < 10 or > 25 breaths/min
Blood Pressure Systolic: < 90 or > 180 mmHg
Diastolic: < 50 or > 110 mmHg
SpO₂ < 92% (or < 88% for COPD)

Important Guidelines:

  • Do not take blood pressure on an arm with a fistula or AV graft
  • Do not take blood pressure on arm on side of mastectomy
  • Report significant changes even if within normal ranges
  • Always consider patient's baseline values

Common Rhythms

Normal Sinus Rhythm

  • Regular rhythm
  • Rate 60-100 bpm
  • P wave before each QRS
  • Normal PR interval
  • Normal QRS duration

Sinus Tachycardia

  • Regular rhythm
  • Rate > 100 bpm
  • Normal P waves
  • Normal PR interval
  • Normal QRS duration

Sinus Bradycardia

  • Regular rhythm
  • Rate < 60 bpm
  • Normal P waves
  • Normal PR interval
  • Normal QRS duration

Atrial Fibrillation

  • Irregularly irregular rhythm
  • No discernible P waves
  • Wavy baseline
  • Normal QRS duration

Ventricular Tachycardia

  • Regular, wide QRS complexes
  • Rate > 100 bpm
  • No visible P waves
  • Medical emergency - prepare for code response

IV Infusion Calculations

Procedures

IV Flow Rate Formulas

mL/hr Calculation

Flow rate (mL/hr) = Total volume (mL) ÷ Time (hours)

Drops per Minute Calculation

gtts/min = (mL/hr × Drop factor) ÷ 60

Common Drop Factors

IV Set Type Drop Factor (gtts/mL)
Macrodrip 10, 15, or 20
Microdrip 60
Blood Administration 10 or 15
Flip card for more calculations

IV Completion Time

Time (hours) = Total volume (mL) ÷ Flow rate (mL/hr)

Example Calculations

Example 1: Flow Rate

Order: 1000 mL NS over 8 hours

Flow rate = 1000 mL ÷ 8 hr = 125 mL/hr

Example 2: Drops/Minute

Flow rate: 125 mL/hr

Drop factor: 15 gtts/mL

gtts/min = (125 × 15) ÷ 60 = 31.25 ≈ 31 gtts/min

Example 3: Completion Time

Volume: 500 mL

Flow rate: 75 mL/hr

Time = 500 ÷ 75 = 6.67 hours

6.67 hours = 6 hours and 40 minutes

Important Tips:

  • Always check the drop factor on the IV set packaging
  • Count drops for a full minute for accuracy
  • Regularly check infusion progress
  • Ensure IV pumps are programmed correctly

SBAR Communication

Communication

SBAR Framework

Structured communication tool for effective handovers and reporting:

Component Description
S - Situation Brief statement of the current situation or problem
B - Background Relevant clinical background or context
A - Assessment Your analysis and considerations of the situation
R - Recommendation Your suggestion for what needs to be done
Flip card for SBAR examples

SBAR Example: Deteriorating Patient

Situation: "Dr. Lee, I'm calling about Mr. Tan in Bed 4. His oxygen saturation has dropped to 88% on room air and he's becoming more short of breath."

Background: "Mr. Tan is a 68-year-old man admitted yesterday with pneumonia. He was on 2L oxygen via nasal cannula with SpO₂ 95%. He has a history of COPD and diabetes. His last set of vitals 30 minutes ago showed temperature 38.2°C, HR 112, BP 135/85, RR 24."

Assessment: "I think he's experiencing respiratory distress related to his pneumonia. His work of breathing has increased, and he's using accessory muscles. He's also becoming anxious and agitated."

Recommendation: "I'd like you to see him now and consider increasing his oxygen therapy. He may need arterial blood gases and possibly chest physiotherapy or nebulization."

Tips for Effective SBAR

  • Be concise and stick to relevant information
  • Prepare your SBAR before contacting the provider
  • Have patient information and vital signs ready
  • Clearly identify the reason for communication
  • Document the SBAR communication and response
  • Use SBAR in emergency situations to ensure clarity
  • Be specific about when you need a response

Note: SBAR is a standard communication tool used in all Singapore healthcare institutions to improve patient safety and reduce communication errors.

Assessments

Stool Form Classification

Type Description Indication
Type 1 Separate hard lumps, like nuts Severe constipation
Type 2 Sausage-shaped but lumpy Mild constipation
Type 3 Sausage-like with cracks on surface Normal
Type 4 Sausage or snake-like, smooth and soft Normal (ideal)

Flip card for clinical application

Clinical Applications

Classification Clinical Significance
Types 1-2
  • Constipation
  • Increased risk for impaction
  • May indicate need for laxatives
Types 3-4
  • Normal bowel function
  • Ideal stool consistency
  • Easy to pass
Types 5-7
  • Progressively more diarrhea
  • Increased risk for dehydration
  • May indicate infection, malabsorption, or medication side effects

Documentation Guidelines

Document the following information:

  • Bristol Stool Chart type (1-7)
  • Frequency of bowel movements
  • Color of stool
  • Presence of blood, mucus, or undigested food
  • Associated symptoms (pain, bloating, straining)
  • Changes from patient's normal pattern

Nursing Interventions:

  • For Types 1-2: Encourage fluid intake, fiber-rich diet, mobility
  • For Types 5-7: Monitor for dehydration, administer prescribed medications, document intake and output
  • Report significant changes in stool pattern to the physician

Documentation Guidelines

Documentation

General Principles of Documentation

  • Be factual, objective, and accurate
  • Document in a timely manner
  • Follow institutional policies and formats
  • Use only approved abbreviations
  • Sign all entries with name and designation
  • Date and time all entries
  • Ensure legibility and correct grammar
  • Document only what you observe or do
  • Never leave blank spaces or lines
  • Correct errors properly according to policy

What to Document

  • Patient assessments
  • Interventions performed
  • Patient responses to care
  • Changes in condition
  • Medications administered
  • Patient education provided
  • Communication with healthcare team

Color Coding for Documentation

Singapore hospitals often use color-coded documentation:

  • Blue ink: Day shift (7am-3pm)
  • Green ink: Evening shift (3pm-11pm)
  • Red ink: Night shift (11pm-7am)
  • Black ink: May be used across all shifts in some facilities

Legal Considerations

  • Documentation is a legal record
  • Adhere to Singapore Nursing Board guidelines
  • Follow the "if it's not documented, it wasn't done" principle
  • Properly document all unusual occurrences or incidents
  • Document patient refusals of care
  • Never alter records after the fact
  • Use quotation marks for direct patient statements

Note: Electronic Health Records (EHR) are increasingly common in Singapore healthcare institutions. Ensure you are properly trained on your facility's EHR system.

OLD CART Assessment

Assessments

Symptom Assessment Framework

OLD CART is a mnemonic used to assess symptoms systematically:

Component Assessment Question
O - Onset When did the symptom begin? Sudden or gradual?
L - Location Where is the symptom? Does it radiate?
D - Duration How long does the symptom last?
C - Characteristics How would you describe the symptom?
A - Aggravating Factors What makes the symptom worse?
R - Relieving Factors What makes the symptom better?
T - Treatment What treatments have you tried?
Flip card for clinical examples

Example: Chest Pain Assessment

Component Sample Documentation
Onset "Started suddenly while watching TV 2 hours ago"
Location "Central chest pain radiating to left arm and jaw"
Duration "Continuous pain for past 2 hours"
Characteristics "Crushing, heavy pressure sensation, 8/10 intensity"
Aggravating Factors "Worse with movement and deep breathing"
Relieving Factors "No relief with rest or position change"
Treatment "Took 2 antacid tablets without relief"

Additional Assessment Areas

  • S - Severity: Pain scale rating (0-10)
  • A - Associated Symptoms: Other symptoms occurring simultaneously
  • T - Timing: Pattern, frequency, time of day

Tips for Effective Assessment:

  • Use open-ended questions
  • Document using patient's own words when appropriate
  • Complete all components of OLD CART
  • Use OLD CART for all symptom assessments for consistency
  • Focus on changes from baseline or previous assessments
